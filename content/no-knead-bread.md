There is a particular kind of satisfaction in pulling a loaf of bread out of the oven that you barely touched. No-knead bread asks almost nothing of you — a few minutes of mixing, a long rest, and a hot oven — and gives back a crackly, golden crust with a soft, airy crumb that feels like it shouldn't be possible from so little effort.

I first tried this after reading about Jim Lahey's method, the one that swept through home kitchens in the mid-2000s. I was skeptical. Bread without kneading felt like cheating, like skipping the part that actually mattered. But the science is straightforward: a long, slow fermentation develops gluten just as effectively as ten minutes of muscling dough around on a counter. Time does the work. You just have to let it.

## What you need

- 400g bread flour (plain works too, but bread flour gives a better chew)
- 300g water, at room temperature
- 8g salt
- 2g instant yeast (about half a teaspoon — less than you think)

That's it. Four ingredients. You probably have them right now.

## The method

Combine the flour, salt, and yeast in a large bowl. Add the water and stir with a wooden spoon or a spatula until everything comes together into a shaggy, rough mass. It will look messy. It will look like it's not going to become bread. That's fine. Cover the bowl with cling film or a tea towel and leave it on your counter for 12 to 18 hours. I usually mix it before bed and come back to it the next morning.

When you return, the dough will have transformed. It'll be bubbly, sticky, and dramatically larger. Flour your hands and a work surface, then gently tip the dough out. Fold it over itself a few times — north to south, east to west — just enough to give it some shape. Don't handle it more than you need to. Form it into a rough ball and place it seam-side down on a piece of parchment paper. Cover loosely and let it rest for another hour or two, until it looks puffy and relaxed.

About thirty minutes before you're ready to bake, put a Dutch oven (with its lid on) into your oven and preheat to 230°C. You want that pot screaming hot.

When it's time, carefully lift the parchment and lower the dough into the Dutch oven. Put the lid back on and bake for 30 minutes. Then remove the lid and bake for another 15 to 20 minutes, until the crust is deep golden brown and the loaf sounds hollow when you tap the bottom.

Let it cool on a wire rack for at least 30 minutes. I know. It smells incredible and waiting is genuinely difficult. But cutting into bread before it's finished cooling ruins the crumb — the interior is still setting up, and all that steam needs somewhere to go.

## Why this matters

This bread won't win any artisan baking competitions. The shape is rustic at best, lopsided at worst. But it tastes like real bread — the kind with actual flavour, a crust that shatters, and an inside that tears into soft, irregular pieces. It costs almost nothing to make. And the process itself is a good reminder that some of the best food comes not from technique or expensive equipment, but from patience and a willingness to leave things alone.

Make it once. You'll make it again.
