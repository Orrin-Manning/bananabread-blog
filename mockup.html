Bread & Baking

The No-Knead Loaf That Changed How I Think About Time

Eighteen hours is not a long time. It just feels that way when you're waiting for bread — and then it comes out of the oven and you understand why you waited.

By Orrin Manning  ·  Saturday, 14 March 2026

The first time I made no-knead bread I didn't believe the recipe. Four ingredients. No kneading. Leave it overnight. That's it. It seemed like the kind of thing someone writes when they want to feel clever about doing very little.

I was wrong. The bread came out with a crust that shattered when I tapped it — the kind of sound that makes you feel like you've done something right. The crumb was open and irregular in the way that supermarket bread never is, which turns out to be the whole point.

"The fermentation is the work. You're just the person who remembers to start it the night before."

What the long rise does is give time for flavour to develop in a way that fast commercial yeast simply cannot replicate. The bacteria and wild yeast do their work slowly, producing the acids and esters that make bread taste like something rather than nothing.
